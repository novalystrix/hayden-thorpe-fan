The Man Behind the Voice

From the windswept hills of Cumbria to the stages of the world — and back into himself.

Hayden Norman Thorpe was born on 18 January 1986 in Kendal, Cumbria — a small market town in England's Lake District, better known for its mint cake than its musicians. But from this unassuming corner of northern England emerged one of indie rock's most distinctive and daring voices.

At sixteen, while still a student at Queen Katherine School, Thorpe formed a duo called Fauve (French for "wild beast") with classmate Ben Little. That teenage experiment would evolve into Wild Beasts — a band that spent sixteen years challenging every assumption about what British indie music could sound like.

"I'm either hyper-specialised or completely maladjusted — or maybe both at the same time."

Thorpe's voice is his signature: a soaring, operatic countertenor that critics have called "Marmite" — you either fall in love instantly or need time to let it in. He once admitted: "I never realised my voice was like Marmite." That vulnerability, combined with lyrics that tackled toxic masculinity, latent sexuality, and the strange tenderness of modern life, made Wild Beasts one of the most written-about bands of their generation — even as mainstream success remained just out of reach.

Today, Thorpe lives in Walthamstow, East London, where he continues to write, record, and explore the space between beauty and strangeness. He has released three solo albums, each more adventurous than the last — from the piano-led soul-searching of Diviner to the nature-inspired soundscapes of Ness, created with thistles, seeds, and grasses collected from a former weapons testing site on the Suffolk coast.

The Journey

Two decades of reinvention — from Kendal schoolboys to one of British music's most compelling stories.

2002
The Beginning: Fauve

Two sixteen-year-olds at Queen Katherine School in Kendal — Hayden Thorpe and Ben Little — form a duo called Fauve. They start writing songs in bedrooms and borrowed rehearsal spaces, unaware they're planting the seed of something that will last sixteen years.

2004
Wild Beasts Are Born

Classmates Chris Talbot (drums) and Gareth Bullock (bass) join. The name changes to Wild Beasts. They record their first demo EP in a space called "Studio 6" in Kendal. The sound is already peculiar and magnetic — Hayden's falsetto cutting through like nothing else in British music.

2005
Leeds Calling

The band relocates to Leeds. Tom Fleming replaces Bullock on bass, completing the classic lineup. Their first Leeds gig is at Trash (formerly The Mixing Tin). The university town becomes their proving ground.

2006
First Record Deal

Wild Beasts sign to Bad Sneakers Records and release "Brave Bulging Buoyant Clairvoyants" — a title that tells you everything about their boldness. The single hits #17 on the UK indie chart. NME and Marc Riley's BBC Radio 6 Music take notice. The Guardian describes Hayden's "enormous falsetto voice, which soars over chiming guitars."

2007
Domino Records

The big leap: Wild Beasts sign to Domino Records, home to Arctic Monkeys and Franz Ferdinand. NME names them one of ten bands "tipped for the top." The trajectory is set.

2008
Limbo, Panto

Their debut album arrives — described as "shocking, funny, and above all irrevocable." It's art-rock with teeth: theatrical, literate, and completely unlike anything their peers are making. The album establishes their reputation as fearless outsiders.

2009
Two Dancers — The Breakthrough

Their masterpiece. Two Dancers lands on practically every "best of" list for 2009 and earns a nomination for the Mercury Prize — British music's most prestigious award. It's the sound of a band finding the perfect balance between beauty and danger, Hayden's voice at its most ravishing.

2011
Smother — Going Deeper

The third album reaches UK No. 17 — their first Top 20. They win the London Awards for Art and Performance. Touring member Katie Harkin (Sky Larkin) joins. The band is growing, but the sound is becoming more intimate, more electronic, more restless.

2014
Present Tense

UK No. 10 — their highest chart position ever. The album pushes further into synth-pop territory while Hayden's lyrics explore masculinity and desire with unprecedented directness. The critical acclaim continues, but commercial breakthrough remains elusive.

2016
Boy King

The final Wild Beasts album. UK No. 9. It's their most muscular, aggressive record — a deliberate reinvention that channels frustration into something primal. Behind the scenes, the tensions that will end the band are already building.

2017–18
The Beautiful End

On 25 September 2017, Wild Beasts announce their disbandment. They release the farewell EP "Punk Drunk & Trembling" and play their final shows — three sold-out nights in Dublin, Manchester, and London. Hayden calls them "a public funeral." The live album "Last Night All My Dreams Came True" captures the final gig at Rockaway Beach Festival. Sixteen years of music, five albums, one Mercury nomination — done.

2019
Diviner — Rebirth

Just one week after Wild Beasts' last show, during the "Beast from the East" snowstorm, Hayden enters the studio alone. London is covered in white — a blank slate. The result is Diviner, his solo debut: piano-led torch songs about intuition, belief, and mysticism. "I'm a keeper of secrets," he sings. "I'll be your disciple — show me where to go." Domino Records releases it in May to warm critical reception.

2020
Aerial Songs

An EP born from lockdown and solitude — songs written with the whole world grounded. The title speaks to Hayden's ability to find lift even when everything is pulled earthward.

2021
Moondust for My Diamond

His second solo album arrives with "The Universe Is Always Right" — a statement of cosmic surrender. The album deepens his solo voice, finding new textures and a confidence that comes from having already let everything go once.

2024
Ness — Into the Wild

His most ambitious work: a musical interpretation of Robert Macfarlane's book about Orford Ness, a former military weapons testing site on the Suffolk coast now reclaimed by nature. Hayden incorporates the actual sounds of thistles, seeds, and grasses collected from the site. "If you had told me two years ago I would be making this record I would have laughed in your face," he says. It's an album about landscape, memory, and the strange beauty of places humans have abandoned.

In His Own Words

Hayden Thorpe speaks like he sings — with precision, vulnerability, and an eye for beauty in unexpected places.

Music is a form of reincarnation. It doesn't degrade, it doesn't erode; music stays the same. The forces of time don't apply to music.

On the end of Wild Beasts — NME, 2019

The band was my religion and the band was my belief system. Once that went, I had to build my own new belief system through scraps of whatever I could find lying around… mostly YouTube philosophers and Instagram.

On losing his identity — NME, 2019

When you're bouncing ideas off yourself, it's kind of like playing chess against yourself. There can be days sat in your dressing gown thinking about the next move.

On making solo music — NME, 2019

I broke up with myself — the idea of myself as the frontman, the idea of myself as the guy in the gang. Even the idea of me being the singer wasn't really definite. It can be beautiful, that process.

On identity after Wild Beasts — NME, 2019

If you had told me two years ago I would be making this record I would have laughed in your face, but Orford Ness is a very human story. I became so compelled to make something strange and beautiful about a non-human being.

On making Ness — BBC, 2024

There was, I'll put it mildly, a gargantuan abyss, that it was a joy to explore, in truth.

On the void after Wild Beasts — NME, 2019

Things You Might Not Know

Beyond the music — the stories, details, and surprises that make Hayden Thorpe one of the most fascinating people in modern British music.

🏔️

Kendal Mint Cake Country

Hayden grew up in Kendal, Cumbria — a town of 30,000 in England's Lake District. The town is famous for Kendal Mint Cake, an energy bar used by Edmund Hillary's 1953 Everest expedition. It's about as far from the London music scene as you can get in England.

🦊

The Name "Fauve"

Before Wild Beasts, the band was called "Fauve" — the French word for "wild beast" and the root of "Fauvism," the art movement led by Matisse. The name change was practical (there were other Fauves) but the connection to radical art movements was deliberate.

🎵

The Lady Gaga Connection

Wild Beasts remixed Lady Gaga's "You and I" — yes, really. The collision of Hayden's ethereal countertenor with Gaga's pop maximalism is one of the most unexpected crossovers in indie history.

❄️

Beast from the East

Hayden recorded the first Diviner sessions during the February 2018 "Beast from the East" snowstorm — just one week after Wild Beasts' final concert. London was buried in snow. He called it "literally a blank space" — a perfect metaphor for his fresh start.

🌿

Recording with Nature

For the Ness album, Hayden collected thistles, seeds, and grasses from Orford Ness in Suffolk — a former military weapons testing site, now a nature reserve — and incorporated their actual sounds into the record. The album is literally made from the landscape.

📖

The Rimbaud Influence

Hayden cites 19th-century French poet Arthur Rimbaud as a major lyrical influence. Rimbaud wrote all his poetry before age 21, then abandoned literature entirely. The parallel with Hayden's own radical reinvention is hard to miss.

🎹

Gary Barlow Was Right

In an NME interview, Hayden admitted that he always mocked Gary Barlow for complaining about the silence after Take That ended. Then Wild Beasts broke up, and Hayden realized: "It turns out Gary Barlow was right. It does get quiet."

🏫

The Teacher's Son

Wild Beasts drummer Chris Talbot was the son of Robert and Lesley Talbot — the music teachers at Queen Katherine School in Kendal, where the band formed. The school's music department literally created the conditions for the band to exist.

🎤

The Jessie Ware & Elbow Sessions

Beyond Lady Gaga, Wild Beasts also collaborated with Jessie Ware ("Night Light"), Elbow ("Gentle Storm"), and the cult band 2:54 ("You're Early"). They were the musicians' musicians — other artists sought their magic.

🪞

"Marmite Voice"

Hayden's soaring countertenor was famously called "like Marmite" by the press — you either love it or you don't. His response? A genuinely surprised: "I never realised my voice was like Marmite." That kind of unselfconscious singularity is exactly why fans love him.

🤝

Tom Fleming's Parallel Path

After Wild Beasts, bassist Tom Fleming became "One True Pairing" and released his own solo debut in September 2019 — the same year as Hayden's Diviner. Fleming later went folk-influenced with "Endless Rain" (2024). Both ex-frontmen found their separate voices.

🏆

So Close to the Mercury

Two Dancers was nominated for the 2010 Mercury Prize but lost to The xx's debut. It's one of indie music's great "what ifs" — had Wild Beasts won, their career trajectory might have been entirely different.
